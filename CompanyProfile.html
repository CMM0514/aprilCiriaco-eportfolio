A. Nature of the Agency

Occidental Mindoro Electric Cooperative, Inc. (OMECO)

Origins & Mandate

  • 1969: PD 269 created NEA to organize electric cooperatives, targeting total municipal electrification by 1987.
  • 1974: OMECO was organized through Atty. Eufrocino L. Ramos of PECT.

Early Operations

  • March 1974: ₱6.9M initial loan signed with NEA; interim Board led by Dr. Rodrigo M. David.
  • May 7, 1974: Took over San Jose Electric Company for ₱620,000 and started with 9 employees.
  • 1975: GM Engr. Alberto A. Guiang acquired two 350kw gensets; lines were built; 1st Electrification Week was held.

Expansion & Projects

  • 1978: Main office moved to Pulang Lupa, Central.
  • 1980: Mamburao energized; bought Silahis Electric Company for ₱760,000.
  • 1981: Engr. Romeo V. Razo became GM to fix negative loan balance.
  • Dendro Thermal Plant: First in Asia in Paclolo, Magsaysay; later failed due to plantation destruction.

Financial Crisis & Recovery

  • 1983-1984: Near collapse, no NEA aid, wages unpaid.
  • Dec. 25, 1983: Col. Zoilo M. Perez became GM; paid staff from personal funds and refused salary for 6 months.
  • March 3, 1984: Operations resumed; cooperative recovered through frugality. Perez was called “Wonder Lolo.”

Achievements Under Col. Perez (1983-1995)

  • Awards: Best Collection Efficiency 1989 & 1991, Outstanding GM 1990; upgraded from Class D to Class A in 1992.

Later Developments

  • 1995: Alex C. Labrador, first rank-and-file GM, took over.
  • 1996: Botasan, Monte Claro, and Sitio Cebu energized; IPC plant in Bubog went 24/7.
  • 1997: Members voted 444-104 to stay with NEA over CDA to keep tax exemptions.
  • 1999: 25th anniversary celebrated.
  • 2000: Sports Fest held; focus stayed on quality service for remote barrios.

Mission

A distribution utility delivering service in a socially responsible manner for the improvement of the quality of life of the people of mainland Occidental Mindoro.

To be financially stable electric cooperative providing quality, reliable and sustainable electric service using word class state-of-the-art and cost-effective systems and facilities operated by highly competent and motivated officers and employees.

C. History of the Agency

OMECO, or Occidental Mindoro Electric Cooperative, Inc., was organized in 1974 through the initiative of Atty. Eufrocino L. Ramos under Presidential Decree No. 269, which created the National Electrification Administration (NEA) in 1969 to oversee rural electrification nationwide. With an initial ₱6.9 million loan from NEA in March 1974, OMECO’s interim board headed by Dr. Rodrigo M. David purchased the San Jose Electric Company for ₱620,000 and formally took over operations on May 7, 1974.

Under its first General Manager, Engr. Alberto A. Guiang, the cooperative expanded from just nine employees, acquired two 350kw generators, built transmission and distribution lines, and launched aggressive membership drives. Public awareness efforts included the first Electrification Week in 1975 and annual membership meetings that encouraged community involvement through the bayanihan system.

Through the late 1970s and 1980s, OMECO continued expanding its coverage, energizing Mamburao in 1980 and acquiring Silahis Electric Company that same year. The cooperative built its main office in Pulang Lupa, Central in 1978 and experimented with projects like the Dendro Thermal Plant in Paclolo, Magsaysay—the first of its kind in Asia—though the Ipil-Ipil plantation for fuel was later destroyed.

By December 1983, OMECO faced a severe financial crisis with no aid from NEA and unpaid wages. Col. Zoilo M. Perez (Ret.) took over as General Manager, paying employees from his own funds and forgoing his salary for six months. Through strict frugality and employee cooperation, operations resumed in March 1984 and the cooperative eventually recovered, earning Perez the nickname “Wonder Lolo” and multiple awards for collection efficiency and management.

In 1995, Alex C. Labrador became the first rank-and-file employee to rise to General Manager, continuing energization efforts in remote areas like Botasan, Monte Claro, and Sitio Cebu in 1996. A major policy debate arose in 1997 over whether to register with the Cooperative Development Authority (CDA) or remain under NEA. In a membership vote on February 21, 1997, 444 voted to stay with NEA versus 104 for CDA, preserving OMECO’s tax exemptions and commitment to serve non-profitable far-flung barangays.

OMECO celebrated its 25th anniversary in 1999 and by 2000 remained focused on its mission of providing dependable service to member-consumers and lighting up remote communities, guided by its established vision, mission, and goals despite ongoing challenges.
